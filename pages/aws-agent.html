Overview AI Meeting Agent Impact & System
AWS Project Libra · AI Meeting Agent

Designing AI that knows when to speak

With only two months to deliver a working demo, the challenge was not just introducing AI into meetings, but determining when it should speak, and when it should stay silent.

The Business Pressure

This was 2023. Every competitor was shipping AI engagement features. Leadership asked why we weren't leveraging ML capabilities already in the stack.

The risk was clear: ship AI that feels intrusive and erode trust in the product entirely. Our research had already shown 83.9% of users wanted AI summaries, but 13% rejected engagement analysis as "frightening." That gap defined the design problem.

Key Constraints

  • Designing within an existing component library for speed and consistency
  • Integrating summaries from third-party platforms, Zoom, Teams, Chime
  • Connecting to user identity and authorization models
  • Filtering non-essential information to prevent cognitive overload
  • Designing for neurodiverse participants with accessibility-first approach

The Decision Framework

Given the compressed timeline, I focused on delivering a grounded experience rather than exploring speculative AI behaviors, emphasizing clarity, relevance, and participant control. This meant intentionally rejecting several ideas that introduced unnecessary automation or surfaced information without strong context.

Rejected concepts: Real-time engagement scoring (felt surveillance-like in testing). Automatic action-item extraction during the meeting (users wanted control over what gets captured). AI-generated follow-up emails (too much autonomy without review).

What I kept

  • Post-meeting summaries only, never during-meeting interruptions
  • Explicit opt-in for AI features per meeting
  • Action items surfaced as suggestions, not assignments
  • Visible "AI is on" indicator, always dismissible

Designing the AI Panel

I partnered closely with PM and engineering to define feasible demo scope, while coordinating with third-party summary platforms early. The AI panel needed to feel like a quiet participant, not a recorder.

Information hierarchy decisions

  • Summary surfaced after meeting ends, not during
  • Action items listed per person, not globally, to reduce overwhelm
  • Confidence indicators on speaker attribution (AI gets this wrong sometimes)
  • One-tap "edit before sending" before any summary leaves the room

The outcome: Demoed and shipped in 2 months. Beta program NPS stayed above 85 throughout. VP endorsement: "This is better than our OP1 executive rooms, can we deploy in all leadership spaces?"
